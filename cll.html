The vacation spot was nothing short of magical. Towering mountains wrapped in silvery cloaks of mist stretched endlessly, their peaks kissed by the heavens. Wildflowers in every imaginable hue blanketed the slopes, as if nature had spilled its palette over the landscape. It felt as though the world had paused to give these children, so accustomed to the gray walls of the orphanage, a glimpse of untouched paradise.

“Look, Sister Rosemary,” Anna whispered, her voice soft with wonder. “The clouds are sitting on the flowers!”

Sister Rosemary chuckled, resting a gentle hand on Anna’s shoulder. “It does seem that way, doesn’t it? God’s handiwork is extraordinary, my dear.”

Before Anna could reply, a loud and impatient voice shattered the moment. “Get off my way, Anna! I’m walking!” Frank’s words were sharp and grating, carrying the unmistakable rudeness of a boy used to pushing boundaries.

Startled, Anna instinctively pulled her hands into her lap as Frank brushed past, his face set in a defiant scowl.

“Frank! That is enough!” Sister Rosemary’s voice snapped like a whip. Her usual calm was replaced with a steely edge. “Do you think Father would approve of you behaving like this?”

Frank froze mid-step, a flicker of guilt flashing across his face. “I’m... I’m sorry, Sister Rosemary,” he muttered, though his tone was far from sincere. His mischievous grin returned almost immediately as his eyes darted to Anna’s wheelchair, a glint of arrogance still lingering.

“Sorry isn’t a word you toss around, Frank. It’s something you mean,” Sister Rosemary said, her gaze fixed firmly on him. “Now behave yourself, or there will be consequences.”

“Yes, Sister,” Frank mumbled, looking away as he kicked at a patch of grass.

“Now, all of you,” Sister Rosemary addressed the group, her voice once again calm but commanding, “head into the house. Mr. Yates has been waiting to meet you.”

The children, their ages ranging from ten to eleven, scattered like birds, racing across the expansive lawn toward the house. The structure loomed ahead—a weathered but proud two-story wooden building. Its paint had faded with time, but the intricate carvings

on the shutters and the grand arch over the door spoke of a past filled with care and craftsmanship. Surrounding the house was an enormous garden, wild and overgrown in parts, but bursting with colors and scents that seemed to echo the mountains’ beauty.

As the children crowded near the heavy oak door, their excitement was palpable. Whispers passed between them, speculating about the mysterious Mr. Yates.

“Do you think he’s scary?” one boy asked, his eyes wide.

“Scary? Nah,” another replied. “He probably just wants to tell us boring rules.”

“Quiet down, all of you!” Sister Rosemary said, sweeping past them and placing a hand on the door’s iron handle. “Whatever Mr. Yates has to say, you’ll listen respectfully. Now, inside.”

The door creaked open, revealing a dimly lit hallway with polished wooden floors and walls lined with faded portraits. The air smelled faintly of lavender and something older, like time itself had left its mark on the place.

Anna craned her neck to look inside, her curiosity piqued. “Sister, who is Mr. Yates?” she asked softly.

“You’ll see soon enough, Anna,” Sister Rosemary said with a small smile, her tone carrying a hint of mystery. “Now, let’s not keep him waiting. He must be an interesting man to meet.”

Inside the old house, the children gathered in the living room, their chatter fading as they noticed the man standing by the fireplace. Mr. Yates, tall and wiry, looked at them with a furrowed brow and a slight

sneer tugging at his weathered face. His coat hung heavily from his shoulders, and his boots, still damp with mud, left faint marks on the wooden floor.

“Finally,” he muttered, his tone sharp. “You’re all here. Took long enough.”

Sister Rosemary stepped forward, her serene expression barely faltering. “Children, this is Mr. Yates. He’s kindly offered to share some important advice about staying safe in St. Hallow’s Valley.”

“Kindly?” Yates barked a laugh. “I wouldn’t say kindly, Sister. I’m doing this because someone has to. These kids need to understand the rules, or they’ll end up like the others.”

The children exchanged uneasy glances, their excitement quickly dampened by the man’s gruff demeanor.

“What others?” Frank asked, his usual bravado tempered by nervous curiosity.

Yates shot him a sharp look. “The ones who didn’t listen,” he snapped. “The ones who thought they were smarter than the fog.” He pointed toward the window, where the mist swirled thickly outside. “That stuff out there? It’s not just fog. It’s alive, in its own way. And it doesn’t like intruders.”

Anna shifted uncomfortably in her wheelchair, her small hands gripping the armrests. “What does it do?” she asked softly.

Yates sighed heavily, as if he found the question tiresome. “What does it do?” he repeated, shaking his head. “It tricks you, that’s what. Changes how it looks, how it feels. The fog is not the same always. Often, it

gets heavier, thicker. Makes it hard to breathe. And then come the sounds.”

“Sounds?” Sister Rosemary prompted, her tone calm but firm.

“Yes, sounds,” Yates said curtly, his tone sharp as he gestured with impatience. “Voices whispering your name. Laughter from no one you know. Sometimes it’s cries, desperate and pleading. Other times, it’s worse—it’s your loved ones. And then, when it really wants to mess with you, it’s your own voice. Mocking, calling, begging. The trick is, you won’t even realize it’s you until it’s too late.” He shot Frank a cold, piercing glare as the boy opened his mouth to speak. “And before you even think about asking, no—you don’t follow them. Not ever. Do I make myself clear?” Frank closed his mouth, sinking back into the group.

“But why—” Anna started.

“Why, why, why,” Yates interrupted, rolling his eyes. “Because those who do don’t come back. That fog is a door, a trap, a—” He waved his hand as if dismissing the need to explain further. “Doesn’t matter what it is. What matters is you stay out of it.”

The room grew quiet, the only sound the faint crackle of the fire in the hearth.

“But there’s one thing more important than anything else,” Yates continued, his tone sharp. He jabbed a finger toward the small altar where a candle burned before a crucifix. “That candle? It stays lit. No excuses, no exceptions. You see it going out, you relight it. Got it?”

“What happens if it goes out?” Anna asked, her voice small but steady.

Yates shot her a dark look. “You don’t want to know,” he snapped. “Let’s just say it won’t be me coming to find you.”

The children huddled closer together, their eyes fixed on the flickering flame of the candle. Even Sister Rosemary seemed uneasy, though she quickly masked it with a reassuring smile for the children.

“Thank you, Mr. Yates,” she said, her voice smooth and composed. “We’ll make sure to follow your advice.”

Yates grunted, clearly unconvinced. “You’d better,” he muttered, turning away and pacing toward the window. He peered out at the swirling fog, his shoulders tense. “Because once it starts, there’s no stopping it.”

A sudden gust rattled the windows, and the flame of the candle flickered. Yates turned sharply, his expression a mix of irritation and unease. “Keep that thing steady,” he barked, pointing at the candle. “I’m not repeating myself.”
